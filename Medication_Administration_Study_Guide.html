📖 Basics of Medication

What is Medication?

A substance administered for the diagnosis, cure, treatment, or relief of a symptom or for prevention of disease.

Pharmacology

The study of the effect of drugs on living organisms.

Prescription

The written direction for the preparation and administration of a drug.

Purpose of Medication

Diagnostic

To identify any disease (e.g. radio contrast dye, Mantoux test)

Prophylaxis

To prevent disease (e.g. heparin for thrombosis, antibiotics)

Therapeutic

To cure the disease

Treatment

Alleviate symptoms (e.g. anti-asthmatic drugs)

Cure

Complete eradication (e.g. antibiotics, anthelmintics)

Enhancement

Best health state (e.g. vitamins, minerals)

Drug Names

Generic Name ▼

Given by the manufacturer who first develops the drug. It is the name by which the drug will be known throughout the world, agreed upon by the WHO. Usually derived from the chemical name.

Example: acetaminophen

Brand / Trade Name ▼

The name given by the pharmaceutical company that manufactures the drug. It is copyrighted — only that company can use that name.

Example: Panadol, Tylenol (both are acetaminophen)

Chemical Name ▼

The precise chemical composition of the drug. Usually complex and not commonly used in clinical practice.

Example: N-(4-hydroxyphenyl)acetamide

Legal Aspects

⚖️ Dangerous Drug Act – 1930

Governs the procurement and use of some drugs especially narcotics (e.g. morphine, pethidine, cocaine). Prescription only — cannot be bought without a prescription. Kept under lock and key in the Dangerous Drug Cupboard under trusted senior nurses.

⚖️ Narcotic Drugs & Psychotropic Substances Act – 1985

Controls psychotropic substances. Strict documentation required for all controlled substances.

Medication Order – 7 Essential Parts

A drug order must contain:

  1. Patient's full name
  2. Date and time
  3. Drug name
  4. Dosage
  5. Route of administration
  6. Time and frequency of administration
  7. Signature of physician

✅ Rights of Medication Administration

⚠️

Medication errors can be detrimental to patients. Always verify ALL rights before administering any medication.

💡 Mnemonic – Remember the Rights

P · D · D · R · T · D · R

Patient · Drug · Dose · Route · Time · Documentation · Reason

1

Right Patient

Ask client to state full name. Compare with ID bracelet and medication chart.

2

Right Drug

Check label 3 times: when removing, preparing, and administering.

3

Right Dose

Calculate carefully. When in doubt, consult another nurse or pharmacist.

4

Right Route

Ensure the ordered route is appropriate and the patient can tolerate it.

5

Right Time

Administer at the scheduled time. Check frequency (OD, BD, TDS, QID).

6

Right Documentation

Record immediately after administration — never before.

7

Right Reason / Education

Know why the patient is receiving the medication. Educate the patient about their medication.

📋 The "Three Checks" Rule

  • Check 1: When removing the medication from the shelf/drawer
  • Check 2: When preparing the medication (drawing up, calculating)
  • Check 3: Before actually administering to the patient

Types of Medication Orders

  • Standing/Routine Order: Carried out as specified until cancelled (e.g. Tab. Paracetamol 500mg TDS)
  • PRN Order (Pro re nata): Given only when required by the patient
  • Single/One-time Order: Given only once at a specific time
  • Stat Order: Given immediately (emergency)
  • Verbal Order: Given by phone/verbally — must be written and countersigned within 24 hours

💉 Routes of Administration

Oral (PO)

Swallowed through the mouth. Most common and safest route.

Angle: N/A

Forms: Tablets, capsules, liquids, syrups

Advantages: Convenient, economical, safe

Sublingual (SL)

Placed under the tongue. Rapid absorption directly into bloodstream.

Example: Glyceryl trinitrate (GTN) for angina

Do NOT swallow or chew the tablet.

Intravenous (IV)

Into a vein. Fastest route — immediate effect.

Angle: 15–30°

Used for emergencies, fluids, antibiotics.

Intramuscular (IM)

Into the muscle tissue.

Angle: 90°

Volume: 2–5 ml (adult)
Sites: Deltoid, vastus lateralis, gluteus

Subcutaneous (SC)

Into the subcutaneous fat tissue, just below skin.

Angle: 45°

Volume: 0.5–1 ml
Example: Insulin, heparin

Intradermal (ID)

Into the dermis (just below skin surface).

Angle: 10–15°

Volume: 0.01–0.1 ml
Example: Mantoux (TB) test, allergy testing

Topical

Applied to skin or mucous membranes.

Forms: Creams, ointments, patches, drops

Local effect. Minimal systemic absorption.

Inhalation

Inhaled through mouth/nose into respiratory tract.

Example: Bronchodilators, anaesthetic gases

Rapid absorption via large lung surface.

Rectal / Suppository

Inserted into the rectum.

Used when patient is NPO, vomiting, or unconscious.

Example: Paracetamol suppository

Epidural / Intrathecal

Epidural: Into epidural space (spine)

Intrathecal: Into subarachnoid space or brain ventricle

Used for spinal anaesthesia, pain relief.

💡 Injection Angles Memory Aid

ID (into skin) = 10–15° (almost flat, like barely entering)  |  SC (below skin) = 45° (half angle)  |  IM (into muscle) = 90° (straight in)

Syringe & Needle Parts

🩺 Syringe Parts

  • Barrel: Graduated cylinder that holds the medication
  • Plunger: Piston that moves to draw or inject
  • Tip: Where the needle attaches

🪡 Needle Parts

  • Hub: Fits onto the tip of the syringe
  • Shaft: Connects to the hub
  • Bevel: The slanted tip of the needle

All needles are made of stainless steel and are disposable.

IV Therapy – Complications to Watch

Infiltration ▼

Escape of fluid into subcutaneous tissue due to needle dislodgement. Causes swelling and pain. May cause nerve compression. Action: Stop infusion and change site immediately.

Phlebitis ▼

Inflammation of the vein. Signs: redness, warmth, swelling along the vein. Action: Discontinue IV, apply warm compress, restart at new site.

Air Embolism ▼

Air enters the bloodstream. Can be life-threatening. Prevention: Always purge air from IV lines before connecting to patient.

Speed Shock ▼

Systemic reaction when IV medication is given too rapidly. Signs: flushing, headache, irregular pulse, shock. Prevention: Always administer at prescribed rate.

IM Injection – Safe Sites

Deltoid

Upper arm. Small muscle — limited to 1–2 ml. Used for vaccines in adults.

Vastus Lateralis

Outer thigh. Preferred for infants and children. Safe (no major nerves or vessels).

Ventrogluteal

Hip area. Safest for adults — no major vessels or nerves nearby. Can take up to 3 ml.

Dorsogluteal

Buttock. Risk of sciatic nerve injury — use with caution. Locate correctly using anatomical landmarks.

🧮 Dosage Calculations

Basic Formula

Dose Required ÷ Dose on Hand × Volume on Hand

Ratio & Proportion Method

A ratio indicates the relationship between two numbers separated by a colon (:).

Example: the ratio 1:2 is the same as 1/2

Three ways to write a proportion:

  • 1:2 = 4:8
  • 1:2 :: 4:8
  • 1/2 = 4/8

Rule: Means × Means = Extremes × Extremes (cross-multiply)

Practice Examples

Example 1 – Tablet

Order: Paracetamol 1000 mg PO
Available: Paracetamol 500 mg tablets

Calculation: 1000 ÷ 500 × 1 = 2 tablets

Example 2 – Liquid

Order: Amoxicillin 250 mg PO
Available: Amoxicillin 125 mg per 5 ml

Calculation: 250 ÷ 125 × 5 = 10 ml

Example 3 – Injection

Order: Morphine 8 mg IM
Available: Morphine 10 mg per 1 ml

Calculation: 8 ÷ 10 × 1 = 0.8 ml

Weight-Based Dosing

Formula: Dose per kg × Patient weight = Total dose

Example: Amoxicillin 25 mg/kg for a 20 kg child
= 25 × 20 = 500 mg

IV Drip Rate Calculation

Drops/min = (Volume in ml × Drop factor) ÷ Time in minutes

Common drop factors: 15 drops/ml (standard), 20 drops/ml, 60 drops/ml (microdrip)

Example: 1000 ml over 8 hours, 15 drops/ml
= (1000 × 15) ÷ (8 × 60) = 15000 ÷ 480 = 31 drops/min
